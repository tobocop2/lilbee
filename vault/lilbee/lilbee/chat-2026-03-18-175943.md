**User**: what the hell is a fat cola?

**Assistant**: A **fat cola** refers to a dense, resinous cluster of cannabis buds (also called "colas") that have developed into large, heavy structures due to optimal growing conditions and training techniques. Here's what makes a cola "fat":

1. **Definition of a Cola**:  
   A cola is the terminal bud (the "head") that forms at the end of a cannabis branch. It is the primary site of THC and CBD production. The term "cola" originates from Spanish, meaning "tail," and was used by Mexican growers to describe the dense, resinous buds.

2. **What Makes a Cola "Fat"**:  
   - **Light Exposure**: Fat colas form when growth tips are exposed to direct light and air. Training techniques like **Low-Stress Training (LST)** or **main-lining** bend branches to position colas closer to the light source (the "sweet spot"), accelerating bud development.  
   - **Nutrition and Stress Management**: Proper nutrient balance, hydration, and stress reduction (e.g., avoiding overwatering or nutrient burn) ensure the plant allocates resources to bud growth.  
   - **Training Techniques**: Methods like **topping** (cutting the main stem to encourage multiple colas) or **manifolding** (creating a wide base of colas) maximize the number of fat colas. For example, the context describes a 16-cola manifold setup where each cola is trained to grow 6 inches above the soil.  

3. **Why Fat Colas Matter**:  
   Fat colas are prized for their high potency and yield. The context notes that growers aim to "fill up your space under the light" during the flowering stage to ensure colas receive adequate light. If colas grow too close to the light, they risk burning, so careful training (e.g., bending stems away) is critical.  

In short, a fat cola is a large, dense bud cluster formed through deliberate cultivation practices that optimize light exposure, plant structure, and resource allocation.

**User**: can you give me week by week instructions on mainlining?

**Assistant**: Here’s a **week-by-week guide to mainlining cannabis plants**, based on the context provided (Nugbuckets’ tutorial and grow journal):

---

### **Week 1: Vegetative Setup & Foundation**  
- **Goal**: Establish a healthy, vigorous plant with a strong root system.  
- **Actions**:  
  - Start with **seedlings** (recommended for beginners) or **clones** (ensure they are acclimated and showing vigorous growth).  
  - Provide **optimal light** (e.g., 18–24 hours/day) and **nutrients** (NPK ratios tailored to vegetative growth).  
  - **Avoid overwatering** to prevent root rot.  
  - **Monitor growth**: Ensure the plant is developing **5–6 nodes** (the ideal starting point for mainlining).  

---

### **Week 2: Vegetative Growth & Node Development**  
- **Goal**: Allow the plant to grow to **5–6 nodes** (typically 3–4 weeks of veg).  
- **Actions**:  
  - Continue **vegetative feeding** (high nitrogen).  
  - **Check for stress**: Ensure the plant is not showing signs of nutrient burn, pests, or mold.  
  - **Clones**: If starting from clones, wait until they show **genetic memory** (i.e., stable growth patterns).  

---

### **Week 3: First Topping (Main-Lining Start)**  
- **Goal**: Create the first main cola by topping the plant.  
- **Actions**:  
  - **Top the plant** to the **3rd node** (cut the stem above the 3rd set of leaves).  
  - **Remove all growth below the 3rd node** (cotyledons, fan leaves, and lateral branches).  
  - **Purpose**: This forces the plant to focus energy on the **main cola** (the 3rd node) and encourages lateral branches to grow into **secondary colas**.  
  - **Note**: Avoid topping **young clones** (they are more temperamental and may slow growth).  

---

### **Week 4: Pruning & Training**  
- **Goal**: Shape the plant into a **flat canopy** with multiple colas.  
- **Actions**:  
  - **Use Low-Stress Training (LST)** to bend lateral branches upward, spreading colas evenly under the light.  
  - **Remove suckers** (small branches) that don’t contribute to colas.  
  - **Monitor light exposure**: Ensure colas are not too close to the light (risk of burning) or too far (inefficient energy use).  
  - **Adjust ties/clips** to maintain the desired structure.  

---

### **Week 5: Finalizing the Manifold**  
- **Goal**: Refine the canopy and prepare for flowering.  
- **Actions**:  
  - **Trim excess foliage** to improve air circulation and light penetration.  
  - **Check for colas** (ideally 8–16 colas, depending on the setup).  
  - **Ensure all colas are evenly spaced** and receiving adequate light.  
  - **Avoid over-pruning**: The plant needs time to recover from training.  

---

### **Week 6: Transition to Flowering**  
- **Goal**: Switch to the flowering phase.  
- **Actions**:  
  - **Change light cycle** to **12/12 hours** (light/dark).  
  - **Adjust nutrients**: Shift to bloom-specific nutrients (higher phosphorus/potassium).  
  - **Monitor colas**: Ensure they are developing properly and not stressed by light or training.  
  - **Yield expectations**: With proper mainlining, expect **3.5–5 ounces per plant** (or more with advanced techniques like 16-cola manifolds).  

---

### **Key Notes from Context**:  
- **Mainlining takes 10–21 days extra** compared to natural growth, averaging **14 days**.  
- **Clones vs. seeds**: Clones are more temperamental and require more time to recover from pruning.  
- **Lollipopping**: Remove lower growth to focus energy on upper colas, but it’s often a last-resort fix for poor planning.  
- **Success depends on observation**: The plant will signal when it’s ready to be pruned or bonded (e.g., new growth, node development).  

By following this structured approach, growers can maximize yield and potency through mainlining.
