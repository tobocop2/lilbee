## Getting Started with Coco Coir

**1. Choose Your Coco Coir Source:**
- **Pre-mixed bags**: Buy ready-made mixes like [FoxFarm Cultivation Nation 70/30 Coco Coir and Perlite](https://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B08NR5RV3L?ie=UTF8&psc=1) or [Roots Organics Soilless Hydroponic Coco Media](https://www.amazon.com/Roots-Organics-ROS-Coco-Soilless/dp/B010NQRGAM)
- **Coco bricks**: More economical option like [Plantonix Coco bricks](https://www.amazon.com/Plantonix-Coco-listed-Organic-Bricks/dp/B01N1YP8O6) that need to be rehydrated
- **Loose coco coir**: For amending existing soil

**2. Prepare Your Coco Coir:**
- If using bricks, let them sit in water for at least an hour to fully expand
- Rinse thoroughly to remove excess salt (especially important for bricks)
- Once expanded, break into a dark, rich soil-like consistency
- Mix with perlite (use more perlite in humid environments, less in dry environments)

**3. Recommended Mix Ratio:**
- **70/30 Coco to Perlite** is recommended (50/50 better for high-frequency fertigation)
- Example: [Mother Earth Coco + Perlite Mix](https://www.amazon.com/Mother-Earth-Coco-Perlite-Mix/dp/B09QXTJ345) - one 1.8 cubic ft bag fills about 12 gallons

**4. Start Plants:**
- Use standard garden or nursery pots with drainage holes
- For small setups, [Mother Earth Coco](https://www.amazon.com/Mother-Earth-Coco-Perlite-Mix/dp/B09QXTJ345) 50L bag (about 13 gallons) can fill two solo cups for seedlings, then two 1-gallon pots, then final transplant to two 5-gallon pots

**5. Important Notes:**
- **Always use nutrients with coco** - it's an inert medium that doesn't contain nutrients like soil
- Start with diluted nutrients (1/4 strength) until plants have grown a few sets of leaves
- Use nutrients like [Dyna-Gro Grow + Bloom](https://www.amazon.com/Dyna-Gro-DYNAGB8OZSET-Liquid-Grow-Bloom/dp/B014UAEPVK)
- Water slowly in circles around the base of plants, starting with 2 cups (500ml) per seedling
- Keep grow lights further away initially (double the normal distance)

**6. Resources:**
- [Coco Coir Rehydration Tutorial](https://www.growweedeasy.com/coco-coir#coco-coir-tutorial-what-you-need)
- [Step-By-Step 250W Coco Tutorial](https://www.growweedeasy.com/250w-hps-beginner-tutorial) for beginners

The key difference from soil is that you must provide nutrients from day one since coco coir doesn't contain any nutrients.

## Most Common Causes of Yellow Leaves

**1. pH Imbalance (Most Common)**
- Yellow leaf tips are typically caused by incorrect pH at the roots (too high or too low)
- When pH is wrong, nutrients become harder for plants to absorb
- Symptoms include yellow tips with other unusual colors like bluish-green or purple leaves
- Brown spots and splotches often appear with low pH
- Yellowing usually occurs at the top of the plant where leaves get direct light

**2. Light Stress (Grow Light Too Close)**
- Leaves closest to grow lights turn yellow due to light burn
- Often accompanied by leaves pointing up or curling
- Top leaves may appear pale compared to the rest of the plant
- Yellow tips from light stress are often mistaken for nutrient deficiencies

**3. Overwatering**
- Heavy yellowing all over the plant, especially in seedlings
- Prevents proper water and nutrient movement through the plant
- Can mimic light stress symptoms
- Leaves may be "stuck on" and not easily plucked off

**4. Nutrient Deficiency**
- Usually affects older, lower leaves first (nitrogen deficiency)
- Yellowing that "climbs" up from bottom to top indicates a problem
- However, yellow tips specifically are more commonly pH-related than nutrient deficiency
- Nitrogen deficiency typically shows yellowing starting from the bottom of the plant

**5. Environmental Stress**
- Heat, cold, low humidity, or poor air circulation
- Usually accompanied by other stress symptoms on the plant

The key diagnostic clue is that **yellow tips specifically are usually caused by pH problems or light stress**, not general nutrient deficiencies. If you see yellow tips without other symptoms throughout the plant, check your pH levels and light distance first.
